The Age of Bronze, by Auguste Rodin.
1877.
Bronze.
Current Location: Museé d'Orsay, France

This sculpture by Rodin is so realistic that when he first presented it in Paris, he was accused of using a cast from a live model rather than sculpting by removing. This technique was frowned upon. However, his critics later confirmed that Rodin had indeed sculpted by removing, showcasing his ability to create supple and lucid sculptures even from bronze.
Initially, the name of this sculpture was Le Vaincu (The Vanquished), perhaps to convey the sentiment amidst the Franco-Prussian War. However, Rodin wanted the French government to commission works from him, which may have motivated him to change the name to L'Âge d'Airain (The Age of Bronze). Rodin worked on this piece for 18 months and used photographs of a man named Auguste Neyt as his model.
Unlike Greek and Roman sculptors, Rodin created figures with more natural proportions.
